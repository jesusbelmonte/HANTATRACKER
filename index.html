Current Situation

The Outbreak in Detail

The MV Hondius, a polar expedition cruise ship, became the focal point of one of the most geographically complex hantavirus outbreaks in recent years — a shipborne cluster spanning 22 nationalities, now heading toward European shores under strict medical supervision.

As of May 8, 2026, the vessel is navigating toward the Canary Islands after remaining anchored near Cape Verde while international health authorities coordinated their response. There are 147 people aboard — passengers and crew — from at least 22 different nationalities.

WHO-confirmed outbreak data: 8 total cases identified — 5 confirmed by laboratory as Andes hantavirus, 3 under investigation as suspected cases. Three confirmed deaths. One patient in critical condition remains hospitalized in Johannesburg, South Africa. Remaining affected individuals present mild symptoms and are under medical monitoring.

Among the people aboard are 14 Spanish nationals. Spain's Ministry of Health has confirmed that none of them are presenting symptoms as of this date. Spain is working in coordination with WHO, the United Kingdom, the Netherlands and other European countries to manage the repatriation of their respective nationals.

Passengers will not disembark freely in Tenerife. Under the activated protocols, each country will receive its citizens under preventive quarantine. For Spain, the designated facility is Hospital Gómez Ulla in Madrid, a Ministry of Defence hospital with certified expertise in high biological-risk case management.

Origin hypothesis (under investigation): The World Health Organization considers it likely that exposure to the virus occurred before boarding, possibly during land-based activities in Argentina or neighbouring areas of the Southern Cone. The Andes strain of hantavirus is endemic in that region and has the longest incubation period of any known variant — up to 45 days.

The WHO has explicitly warned that, given the incubation period of up to 45 days, new cases could be identified among passengers and crew over the coming weeks — even among individuals currently presenting no symptoms whatsoever. This makes the monitoring of all 147 people aboard an international health priority.

Global risk assessment: WHO classifies the global health risk associated with this outbreak as "low". Spain's Ministry of Health agrees, rating the risk for the Spanish population as "extremely low". Authorities explicitly rule out the possibility of a COVID-19-style pandemic from this event.

Science of the Outbreak

How Hantavirus is Transmitted

What is known — and what is not yet known — about the Andes strain and its transmission routes.

🐭
Primary transmission routes Confirmed
  • Inhalation of dust contaminated with infected rodent urine, droppings or saliva (primary route).
  • Direct contact with infected rodents or their nesting materials.
  • Rodent bite from a carrier of the virus.
  • Ingestion of food contaminated with rodent excreta (less common).
👥
Person-to-person — Andes strain Under investigation

The Andes strain is the only known hantavirus variant with documented human-to-human transmission. However, this route is considered rare and requires close, prolonged contact with an actively infected person.


WHO does not consider there to be a meaningful transmission risk to the general public under the protocols currently in place.

✅
What does NOT transmit the virus Confirmed
  • Casual contact (handshakes, touching surfaces).
  • Insect bites — no vector insect involved.
  • Hygienically prepared food or water.
  • Long-range airborne transmission (unlike COVID-19).
🏥
Treatment & vaccine Confirmed

No widely available vaccine or approved antiviral treatment exists in Europe or the USA for hantavirus. Treatment is supportive intensive care: oxygenation, blood pressure control and ICU management. Early detection is the single most important survival factor.

Epidemiological Context

Hantavirus Around the World

Hantavirus is not a new pathogen. It circulates endemically in wild rodent populations across multiple world regions. What makes the MV Hondius outbreak singular is its transnational character, the specific strain involved, and the vector of dispersion — a cruise ship carrying passengers from 22 countries.

Strains of greatest epidemiological concern: Andes (South America — only strain with documented H2H transmission) · Sin Nombre (USA, ~36% fatality) · Puumala (Europe, low fatality) · Seoul (global distribution via domestic rats).

Spain has never recorded autochthonous Andes strain cases. Sporadic cases of the Puumala variant — far less lethal — have been documented in other parts of Europe.

Risk Assessment

What Risk Does This Pose?

🛡️

Risk for Spain & Europe: Extremely Low

Spain's Ministry of Health has confirmed the risk to the Spanish population is extremely low. The 14 Spanish nationals aboard present no symptoms as of May 8, 2026. They will be repatriated to Hospital Gómez Ulla in Madrid under preventive quarantine. Spain has never recorded autochthonous Andes hantavirus cases. The WHO classifies global health risk as 'low' and explicitly rules out a pandemic scenario.

❌
FALSE: "Hantavirus will spread like COVID-19"
Hantavirus is not airborne over distance and does not spread casually between people. It lacks the transmissibility characteristics of SARS-CoV-2. Health authorities explicitly rule out a pandemic scenario.
✅
TRUE: Spaniards will disembark under quarantine
Confirmed: the 14 Spanish nationals will not have free access to Tenerife. They will be transferred directly to Hospital Gómez Ulla in Madrid for preventive quarantine and medical monitoring during the full incubation period.
❌
FALSE: "The virus spreads via surface contact"
Hantavirus does not spread through handshakes, touching surfaces or simply sharing a space with an infected person. It requires exposure to infected rodent secretions or, for the Andes strain, very close and prolonged contact.
✅
TRUE: Andes strain can spread between people
Unlike other hantavirus strains, Andes is the only one with documented human-to-human transmission. However, this remains rare, requires prolonged close contact, and does not imply any risk to the general public under the protocols in place.
